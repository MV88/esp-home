User Guide

The tool is an interactive knowledge platform that gathers and makes available a wide range of information on ecosystem services maps, data and mapping methodologies.
The diversity of mapping ecosystem services (ES) approaches, has led to a variety in methods, models, classification systems and terminologies used to communicate the ecosystem services related research and practice. To better manage this diversity and facilitate the dialogue and data sharing among the ES practitioners across levels and disciplines, the need for a system that gathers information on indicators used to map ecosystem services, becomes more and more urgent. This tool builds upon a recent call for improved guidance on modelling and mapping ecosystem services by introducing a so-called ecosystem service mapping blueprint (Crossman et al., 2013). An ecosystem mapping blueprint can be seen as a template and checklist of information needed for those beginning an ecosystem service modelling and mapping study. This tool should become a valuable information resource of methods and information used in previous modelling and mapping studies.
The tool has three main functions:
  • Data This function allows the user to search the database for information on ES maps.
    1. Go to Data
      By selecting Filter you can query the database based on four filter types:
      1. Ecosystem Services (based on TEEB 2010 classification)
      2. Biomes assessed (following the ESP Biome working groups)
      3. Spatial scale of the ES map
      4. Study purpose
    2. View the filtering results
      Click on View Data
      View the list of ES maps that corresponds to the search criteria with basic information on the indicators.
      View and/or download the metadata of one or more ES maps by selecting those of your interest by selecting the left side checkboxes and then clicking on the , or the icon.
      View a description of the study metadata by clicking on the Study name.
      To view a map you can click on the icon.
      To download a map, click on the if the map is a shapefile, or the if the map was uploaded as a GeoTiff.
      Go back to the search result list click on Data.

  • Maps
    This function allows the user to view and/or download the different ecosystem services maps available in the database.
    On the right top corner select the background layer map you wish to view.
    On the right top corner, bellow that turn on/off the map of regions.
    Click on a geographic region on the global map and view the list of ES maps that are available for that region in the database. The regions are coloured depending on the number of available studies per region.
    By filtering the database and clicking on the View map button you can view the available one either as a Geotiff or a shapefile in the database.
    You can only view one map at a time. The map legend and title is at the right side of the screen.
    Change the map opacity by moving the opacity bar in the legend box.
    Download the map in its uploaded format.

  • Upload
    Upload your ecosystem service maps and related metadata in the database.
    1. Login with your own Google, Yahoo, myOpenIs or Stack Exchange account.
    2. Enter information about the ecosystem service mapping study by clicking New. Remember to click on Save for each information entered.
    3. You can modify an existing study entry by clicking on Edit.
    4. For the study add information on the ES map you want to upload. For each indicator you add per study click on the Add button.
    5. Enter information on the ES maps, quantification method and models used, data sources and other related metadata.
    6. By dragging your mouse over each field that needs to be filled, you can see a window with a short description on what each field stands for.
    7. Add your map either in a GeoTiff format or a zipped shapefile. The file size limit is 150 Megabytes. Note that this is optional.
    8. To convert your raster files to GeoTiff see instructions here.

Glossary

  • Ecosystem services (ES): Contributions of ecosystem structure and function -in combination with other inputs- to human well-being (Burkhard et al., 2012). The classification of ES used in this tool is currently the TEEB classification.
  • Ecosystem Service Indicator: : A measure that serves to indicate or give a suggestion of something of interest and is derived from simple or more complicated measures (TEEB, 2010; Egoh et al., 2012). For example an ES indicator for "Climate Regulation" could be "Carbon sequestered per hectare per year".
  • Ecosystem processes: Changes or reactions occurring in ecosystems; either physical, chemical or biological; including decomposition, production, nutrient cycling and fluxes of nutrients and energy (Millennium Ecosystem Assessment, 2005).
  • Ecosystem structures:Biophysical architecture of ecosystems; species composition making up the architecture may vary (TEEB, 2010).
  • Ecosystem functions: : Intermediate between ecosystem processes and services and can be defined as the capacity of ecosystems to provide goods and services that satisfy human needs, directly and indirectly (de Groot et al., 2010).
  • Ecosystem Service supply: refers to the flow of a specific bundle of ecosystem goods and services from a particular area, within a given time period (Burkhard et al., 2012b). It depends on different sets of landscape properties that influence the level of service supply (Willemen et al., 2012).
  • Ecosystem Service demand: is the sum of all ecosystem goods and services currently consumed or used in a particular area over a given time period (Burkhard et al., 2012a).

FAQ

Who is it for?

The tool is a platform for information exchange on ecosystem service maps and indicators, from data, models and methodologies used, to the specific purpose these serve. The target audience are researchers, students and ecosystem services practitioners. The data is also available to the general public. The goal of the tool is to serve as a guide for people who wish to map ecosystem services and allow for an exchange of ideas on methodologies and data used. It is also a tool for gap analysis, allowing researchers to map what has been already done, how and where. That way duplication of work can be avoided and mapping of the ongoing research can be made feasible. Also, the possibility given to the users to comment on the available information, is a way to validate the maps that have already been uploaded and use the tool as a platform that allows for discussions among the ES practitioners.

What the tool is not for

This is not a mapping tool by itself, i.e. it does not provide the users with tools to map ecosystem services. It stores information and links to data and models which can be shared and used for mapping.
